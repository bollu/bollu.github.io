§ Definitions of matroids

A matrioid MMM is a set XXX equipped with an independence set I⊆2XI \subseteq 2^XI⊆2X.
  • The empty set is independent: ∅∈I\emptyset \in I∅∈I.
  • The independence set is downward-closed/closed under subsets: ∀i∈I,∀i′⊆i,i′∈I \forall i \in I, \forall i' \subseteq i, i' \in I∀i∈I,∀i′⊆i,i′∈I.
  • For any independent sets A,B∈IA, B \in IA,B∈I, if ∣A∣\vert A \vert∣A∣ is larger than ∣B∣\vert B \vert∣B∣, then we must be able to add an element from a∈Aa \in Aa∈A into B′≡B∪aB' \equiv B \cup {a}B′≡B∪a such that B′B'B′ is both independent and larger than BBB: B′∈I∧∣B′∣>∣B∣B' \in I \land \vert B' \vert > \vert B \vertB′∈I∧∣B′∣>∣B∣. (The exchange property)

§ Example 1: Linearly independent sets

Let VVV be a vector space. The independent sets III are of the form:
I≡{S⊆V : vectors in S are lineary independent} I \equiv \{ S \subseteq V ~:~ \text{vectors in $S$ are lineary independent} \} I≡{S⊆V : vectors in S are lineary independent}
This is an independence system because the empty set is linearly independent, and subsets of a linearly independent collection of vectors will be linearly independent. The exchange property is satisfied because of linear algebraic reasons.

§ Bases and Circuits

  • Bases are the maximal independent sets of III (ordered by inclusion). On adding an element into a basis element, they will become dependent. They are called bases by analogy with linear algebra.
  • Circuits are minimal dependent sets of III. This comes from analogy with trees: if we remove an element from any circuit (loop) in a graph, what we are left with is a tree.
A matroid can be completely categorized by knowing either the bases or the circuits of that matroid.

§ Unique Circuit property

  • Theorem: Let M≡(E,I)M \equiv (E, I)M≡(E,I) be a matroid, and let S∈I,e∈ES \in I, e \in ES∈I,e∈E such that S∪{e}∉IS \cup \{e \} \not \in IS∪{e}∈I.
Then, there exists a unique circuit C⊆S∪{e}C \subseteq S \cup \{ e \}C⊆S∪{e}. That is, when we go from independent to dependent by adding an element, we will have a single, unique circuit. For example, when we add an edge into a forest to create a cycle, this cycle will be unique!

§ Intersection of matroids is not necessarily a matroid:

M1 = < d {[(a) (b)] c}>

M2 = < {d [(a) (b)]} c>
The intersection of these two matroids will be: 
M1 cap M2 = < d [(a) (b)] c>
This is not a matroid because the exchange property fails. There's no way to go from [a, b] to < d a b c > by exchanging one element.
