§ Operations with modular fractions

  • Quick note on why it's legal to perform regular arithmetic operations on fractions a/ba/ba/b as operations on ab−1ab^{-1}ab−1 where ab−1∈Z/pZab^{-1} \in \mathbb Z/pZab−1∈Z/pZ.
  • The idea is that we wish to show that the map a/b↦ab−1a/b \mapsto ab^{-1}a/b↦ab−1 is a ring homomorphism ϕ:Q→Z/pZ\phi: \mathbb Q \to \mathbb Z/p \mathbb Zϕ:Q→Z/pZ.
  • The proof: (i) the map Z→Z/pZZ \rightarrow Z/pZZ→Z/pZ is a ring homormophism, (ii) map from an integral domain to a field always factors through the field of fractions of the domain, we get a map ϕ:Q→Z/pZ\phi: \mathbb Q \rightarrow \mathbb Z/ p \mathbb Zϕ:Q→Z/pZ. So from abstract nonsense, we see that ϕ\phiϕ will be a well defined ring.hom.
  • More down to earth: let's check addition multiplication, and multiplicative inverse. All else should work automagically.
  • For addition, we wish to show that ϕ(a/b+c/d)=ϕ(a/b)+ϕ(c/d)\phi(a/b + c/d) = \phi(a/b) + \phi(c/d)ϕ(a/b+c/d)=ϕ(a/b)+ϕ(c/d). Perform the calculation:
\begin{aligned} &\phi(a/b + c/d) \\ &=\phi((ad + bc)/bd) \\ &= (ad + bc)(bd)^{-1}\\ &= abb^{-1}d^{-1} + bcb^{-1}d^{-1} \\ &= ad^{-1} + cd^{-1} \\ &= \phi{a/d} + \phi{c/d} \\ \end{aligned}
  • For multiplication, we wish to show that ϕ(a/b⋅c/d)=ϕ(a/b)⋅ϕ(c/d)\phi(a/b \cdot c/d) = \phi(a/b) \cdot \phi(c/d)ϕ(a/b⋅c/d)=ϕ(a/b)⋅ϕ(c/d):
\begin{aligned} &\phi(a/b \cdot c/d) \\ &=\phi{ac/bd} &= ac(bd)^{-1} \\ &= acd^{-1}b^{-1} \\ &= ab^{-1} \cdot cd^{-1} \\ &= \phi{a/b} \cdot \phi{c/d} \\ \end{aligned}
  • For inverse, we wish to show that ϕ(1/(a/b))=ϕ(a/b)−1\phi(1/(a/b)) = \phi(a/b)^{-1}ϕ(1/(a/b))=ϕ(a/b)−1:
\begin{aligned} &\phi(1/(a/b)) &=\phi{b/a} &= ba^{-1} &= (ab^{-1})^{-1} &= \phi(a/b)^{-1} \end{aligned} Thus, we can simply represent terms a/ba/ba/b in terms of ab−1ab^{-1}ab−1 and perform arithmetic as usual.
