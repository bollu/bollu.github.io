The Jacobian

In this article, I'm going to motivate a geometric quantity known as the Jacobian you may have seen from multivariable calculus. It shows up without explanation when one solves multiple integrals; little explanation is given to what it means . At the end of reading this, if you know what a Jacobian is, you'll have a sense of what it really means; If you didn't, that's even better --- you'll learn what the Jacobian is with no misconceptions.

Let's dive in. To discuss the jacobian, we first need a transformation. Let's pick the transformation that squeezes the entire plane into the unit circle. This is given by \( f(v) \equiv v/|v| \) where \( v \) is a 2D vector, or a point on a plane. Written in terms of coordinates, we make the substitution \( v \mapsto (x, y) \), and the length of the vector \( |v| \mapsto \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \). This yield the expression \( f(x, y) \equiv (x/\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, y/\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}) \). to get a very visceral sense of what this function does, let's think about it this way: we distribute pink-colored points randomly , and then transform these points using \( f\). This gives a sense of how \( f \) crumples the unit plane onto the unit circle.

There's another way to view this transform, which will be slightly more fruitful for us in the future; We're going to look at how this function $f$ transforms curves, because a curve is the most primitive object that has notions of continuity, smoothness, and all other nice geometic properties we know and love from calculus. We have a pink curve that moves around; This curve is mapped by \( f \) to the blue curve that lies entirely on the unit circle. Watching this transformation gives us a sense of what geometry the mapping \( f \) preserves, and what \( f \) destroys.

To watch more closely, let's consider two extreme cases. First, where the pink curve is perpendicular to the circle. Here, we see below that that the projection in blue vanishes. Regardless of however long our pink curve is, the projection is always just a single point. A curve that is perpendicular to the circle will have zero projection.

The other case we consider is where the curve is tangent or parallel to the circle. Here, we find the projection of the pink curve to be very long, as the pink curve is able to cast a long shadow onto the circle.

The transformation of derivatives

We now have a handle on how continuity is transformed. Let's now try to understand how tangents or derivatives are transformed, since differentiability comes right after continuity in Calculus. To explore this, let's first draw a pink curve, draw its tangents, see what this curve is mapped to by \( f \), and then draw the mapped curve's tangents. This should give us insight into how the tangents to a curve is transformed by \( f \). But, what even is a tangent? Calculus tells us that it's straight line that just touches our curve. So we'll draw the tangent as a straight line to our curve, then push the tangent onto the circle using \( f \) to see what it looks like after the transformation.

We see that the length of the pink tangent is not constant! As we come closer to the circle, the length of the tangent is longer, since the point moves faster on the circle. As we go farther away from the circle, the length of the tangent grows ever shorter, as the velocity on the point on the circle crawls to a halt.
