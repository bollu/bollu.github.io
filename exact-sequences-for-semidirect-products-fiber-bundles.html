§ Viewing the semidirect product space as a G-bundle

Consider the space E≡N⋉KE \equiv N \ltimes KE≡N⋉K as a bundle over KKK given by the projection E≡N⋉K→πKE \equiv N \ltimes K \xrightarrow{\pi} KE≡N⋉Kπ​K. We can have NNN act on the fibers by a left and a right action. Let's consider both:
  • NNN acting on right: (n,k)◃(n′,e)≡(nn′k,ke)≡(nn′k,k)(n, k) \triangleleft (n', e) \equiv (n n'^k, ke) \equiv (n n'^k, k)(n,k)◃(n′,e)≡(nn′k,ke)≡(nn′k,k)
  • NNN acting on left: (n′,e)▹(n,k)≡(n′ne,k)=(n′n,k)(n', e) \triangleright (n, k) \equiv (n' n^e, k) = (n' n, k)(n′,e)▹(n,k)≡(n′ne,k)=(n′n,k)This is the "easier action" to interpret;it permutes fibers, keeping the base space the same. So this gives a principal bundle action.
  • KKK acting on right: (n,k)◃(e,′k)≡(nek,kk′)=(ne,kk′)=(n,kk′)(n, k) \triangleleft (e, 'k) \equiv (n e^k, kk') = (ne, kk') = (n, kk')(n,k)◃(e,′k)≡(nek,kk′)=(ne,kk′)=(n,kk′).This action is easy, it permutes fibers.
  • KKK acting on left: (e,k′)▹(n,k)≡(enk′,k′k)≡(nk′,kk′)(e, k') \triangleright (n, k) \equiv (e n^{k'}, k'k) \equiv (n^{k'}, kk')(e,k′)▹(n,k)≡(enk′,k′k)≡(nk′,kk′).
So we see that NNN acting on the left gives us an action that permutes inside fibers, and KKK acting on the right gives us an action that permutes the fibers themselves. So we can write this as N▹N⋉K◃KN \triangleright N \ltimes K \triangleleft KN▹N⋉K◃K to capture the base-space bundle-space relationship, perhaps. Also, see that if we quotient N⋉KN \ltimes KN⋉K by the action of G≡NG\equiv NG≡N acting on the left, with the quotient map called [⋅][\cdot][⋅] for orbit equivalence classes, we get N⋉K→[⋅](N⋉K)/N=KN\ltimes K \xrightarrow{[\cdot]} (N \ltimes K)/N = KN⋉K[⋅]​(N⋉K)/N=K, which is isomorphic to our starting picture N⋉K→πKN \ltimes K \xrightarrow{\pi} KN⋉Kπ​K. Hence, it is indeed true that this bundle is a principal GGG-bundle.

§ Relationship to gauges

NOTE: this was written before I knew what a G-bundle is. This is perhaps easier to read, but less useful in hindsight. Let XXX be the space of all states. Let OOO be a group action whose orbits identify equivalent states. So the space of "physical states" or "states that describe the same physical scenario" is the orbit of XXX under OOO, or X/OX/OX/O. Now, the physical space X/OX/OX/O is acted upon by some group GGG. If we want to "undo the quotienting" to have GGG act on all of XXX, then we need to construct G⋉OG \ltimes OG⋉O. GGG is normal here because OOO already knows how to act on the whole space; GGG does not, so OOO needs to "guide" the action of GGG by acting on it. The data needed to construct G⋉OG \ltimes OG⋉O is a connection. Topologically, we have X→X/OX \rightarrow X/OX→X/O and G↷X/OG \curvearrowright X/OG↷X/O. We want to extend this to (G⋉O)↷X(G \ltimes O) \curvearrowright X(G⋉O)↷X. We imagine this as: 
*1| #1 | @1  X
*2| #2 | @2
*3| #3 | @3
  | |  |
  | v  |
* | #  | @ X/H
where the action of HHH permutes amongst the fibers of *, #, @. Next, we have an action of GGG on X/HX/HX/H:

*2| #2 | @2
*3| #3 | @3
  | |  |
  | v  |

We need to lift this action of H the H-orbits. This is precisely the data a connection gives us (why?) I guess the intuition is that the orbits of XXX are like the tangent spaces where X→X/OX \rightarrow X/OX→X/O is the projection from the bundle into the base space, and the GGG is a curve that tells us what the "next point" we want to travel to from the current point. The connection allows us to "lift" this to "next tangent vector". That's quite beautiful. We want the final picture to be:
