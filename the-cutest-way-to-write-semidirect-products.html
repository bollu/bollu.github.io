§ The cutest way to write semidirect products

Given two monoids (M,+,0M)(M, +, 0_M)(M,+,0M​) and (N,×,1N)(N, \times, 1_N)(N,×,1N​), and a homomorphism ϕ:N→End(M)\phi: N \rightarrow End(M)ϕ:N→End(M), where End(M)End(M)End(M) is the endomorphism group of MMM. We will notate ϕ(n)(m)\phi(n)(m)ϕ(n)(m) as n⋅m∈Mn \cdot m \in Mn⋅m∈M. Now the semidirect product M⋉ϕNM \ltimes_\phi NM⋉ϕ​N is the set M×NM \times NM×N equipped with the multiplication rule:
  • (m,n)(m′,n′)=(m+n⋅m′,nn′)(m, n) (m', n') = (m + n \cdot m', nn')(m,n)(m′,n′)=(m+n⋅m′,nn′)
This can also be written down as:
[10mn][10m′n′]=[10m+n⋅m′n×n′] \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ m & n \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ m' & n' \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ m + n \cdot m' & n \times n' \end{bmatrix} [1m​0n​][1m′​0n′​]=[1m+n⋅m′​0n×n′​]
This way of writing down semidirect products as matrices makes many things immediately clear:
  • The semidirect product is some kind of "shear" transform, since that'swhat a shear transformation looks like, matrix-wise.
  • The resulting monoid M⋉ϕNM \ltimes_{\phi} NM⋉ϕ​N has identity (0M,1N)(0_M, 1_N)(0M​,1N​),since for the matrix to be identity, we need the 2nd row to be (0,1)(0, 1)(0,1).
  • The inverse operation if (M,N)(M, N)(M,N) were groups would have to be such that
[10m+n⋅m′n×n′]=[1001] \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ m + n \cdot m' & n \times n' \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} [1m+n⋅m′​0n×n′​]=[10​01​]
Hence:
  • nn′=1nn' = 1nn′=1 implies that n′=1/nn' = 1/nn′=1/n.
  • m+nm′=0m + n m' = 0m+nm′=0 implies that m′=−m/nm' = -m/nm′=−m/n.
which is indeed the right expression for the inverse.
