§ Why quaternions work better

  • We want to manipuate SO(3)SO(3)SO(3). Imagine it like SO(1)SO(1)SO(1).
  • Unfortunately, π1(SO(3))=Z/2Z\pi_1(SO(3)) = \mathbb Z/2\mathbb Zπ1​(SO(3))=Z/2Z. This is a pain, much like rotations of a circle need to be concatenated with modulo, which is a pain.
  • idea for why π1(SO(3))\pi_1(SO(3))π1​(SO(3)) is Z/2Z\mathbb Z/2\mathbb ZZ/2Z: SO(3)SO(3)SO(3) is sphere with antipodal points identified. So a path from the north pole to the south pole on the sphere is a "loop" in SO(3)SO(3)SO(3). Concatenate this loop with itself (make another trip from the south pole to the north pole) to get a full loop around the sphere, which can be shrunk into nothing as π1(S2)\pi_1(S^2)π1​(S2) is trivial. So ns2=ens^2 = ens2=e, where nsnsns is the north-south path in S2S^2S2which is a loop in SO(3)SO(3)SO(3)).
  • Key idea: deloop the space! How? find univesal cover. Lucikly, universal cover of SO(3)SO(3)SO(3) is SU(2)SU(2)SU(2) / quaternions, just as universal cover of SO(1)SO(1)SO(1) is R\mathbb RR.
  • Universal cover also explains why SU(2)SU(2)SU(2) is a double cover. Since π1(SO(3))\pi_1(SO(3))π1​(SO(3)) is Z/2Z\mathbb Z/2ZZ/2Z, we need to deloop "once" to get the delooped space.
  • No more redundancy now! Just store a bloch sphere representation, or a quaternion (store SU(2)SU(2)SU(2)). Just like we can just store a real number for angle and add it.
  • How to go back to SO(3)SO(3)SO(3) or SO(1)SO(1)SO(1)? Move down the universal cover map SU(2)→SO(3)SU(2) \to SO(3)SU(2)→SO(3) or R→SO(1)\mathbb R \to \mathbb SO(1)R→SO(1).
  • This is strange though. Why is R\mathbb RR both the lie algebra and the covering space of SO(1)SO(1)SO(1) ? What about in general?
  • In general, the original lie group SO(3)SO(3)SO(3) and the universal cover SU(2)SU(2)SU(2) both have the same lie algebra . It is only that the lie group has less or more fundamental group.
