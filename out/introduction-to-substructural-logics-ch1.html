§ Introduction to substructural logics: Ch1

§ Terminology

§ Logic as talking about strings

  • The book gives a new (to me) interpretation of rules like X⊢AX \vdash AX⊢A. It says that this can be read as "the string XXX is of type AAA", where type is some chomskian/grammarian sense of the word "type".
  • This means that we think of X;YX ; YX;Y as "concatenate XXX and YYY".
  • This allows one to think of X⊢A→BX \vdash A \to BX⊢A→B as the statement " XXX when concatenated with a string of type AAAproduces a string of type BBB".
  • This is interesting, because we can have judgements like X⊢AX \vdash AX⊢A and X⊢BX \vdash BX⊢B with no problem, we're asserting that the string XXX is of type AAA, BBB. Which, sure, I guess we can have words that are both nouns and verbs, for example.
  • Under this guise, the statement X⊢A∧BX \vdash A \land BX⊢A∧B just says that " XXX is both a noun and a verb".
  • Further, if I say X⊢AX \vdash AX⊢A and Y⊢BY \vdash BY⊢B, then one wants to ask "what is type of X;YX; YX;Y ? we want to say "it is the type AAA next to BBB", which is given by A∘BA \circ BA∘B (or, A⊗BA \otimes BA⊗B in modern notation).
  • This is cool, since it gives a nice way to conceptualize the difference between conjunction and tensoring.

§ Tensor versus conjunction as vector spaces

  • What I got most out of this was the difference between what they call fusion (what we now call tensoring in linear logic) and conjunction.
  • Key idea: Let's suppose we're living in some huge vector space, and the statement X⊢AX \vdash AX⊢A should be read as "the vector XXX lives in the subspace AAA of the large vector space.
  • Then, the rule , XvdashBX vdash BXvdashB entails X⊢A∧BX \vdash A \land BX⊢A∧B means: if XXX lives in subspace AAA and XXX lives in subspace BBB, then XXX lives in the intersection A∩BA \cap BA∩B.
  • On the other hand, the rule X⊢AX \vdash AX⊢A, Y⊢BY \vdash BY⊢B entails X;Y⊢A∘BX ; Y \vdash A \circ BX;Y⊢A∘B means: if XXX lives in subspace AAA, YYY lives in subspace BBB, then the vector X⊗YX \otimes YX⊗Y lives in subspace A⊗BA \otimes BA⊗B.
  • See that in the case of the conjunction, we are talking about the same XXX, just choosing to restrict where it lives ( A∩BA \cap BA∩B)
  • See that in the case of tensor product, we have two elements XXX and YYY, which live in two different subspaces AAA and BBB.

§ Cut and admissibility

  • Cut is the theorem that lets you have lemmas.
  • It says that if X⊢AX \vdash AX⊢A, and Y(A)⊢BY(A) \vdash BY(A)⊢B then Y(X)⊢BY(X) \vdash BY(X)⊢B.
  • I don't understand what this means in terms of the interpretation of "left hand side as values, right hand side as types", or under "left side is strings, right side is types". The rule Y(A)⊢BY(A) \vdash BY(A)⊢B is, at best, some kind of unholy dependently typed nonsense under this interpretation.
  • A theory is cut-admissible if the axioms let you prove cut.
  • In general, a theory is admissible to some axiom AAA if the axioms of the theory allows one to prove AAA.
