§ Defining models for quantification

  • We wish to define M⊨∀x,F(x)M \models \forall x, F(x)M⊨∀x,F(x)
  • A first shot might be M⊨∃x,F(x)M \models \exists x, F(x)M⊨∃x,F(x) iff for every closed term ttt, M⊨F(t)M \models F(t)M⊨F(t).
  • However, see that intuitively, ∃x\exists x∃x ranges over the denotational space , while closed terms range over the image of syntax in the denotation .
  • For example, consider the language of nautrals, which we can interpret over naturals, nonnegative rationals, and reals. So let us think of the formula (F≡∃t,t+t=1)(F \equiv \exists t, t + t = 1)(F≡∃t,t+t=1). If we only allow ttt to take on closed terms, then see that since the closed terms of naturals are natural numbers, this will be false! But really, when interpreted over the integers, we want the formula to be true, since there is the real number 1/21/21/2 which witnesses the truth of (∃t,t+t=1)(\exists t, t + t = 1)(∃t,t+t=1). Thus, it is insufficient to range over closed terms, since the "image" of the closed terms in R\mathbb RR is going to be N\mathbb NN, but in fact, we have "more in R\mathbb RR" than just the closed terms which are unreachable.
  • So the correct notion of M⊨∃x,F(x)M \models \exists x, F(x)M⊨∃x,F(x) is to take MMM, extend with a constant symbol ccc, evaluate it to some m∈Mm \in Mm∈M, and call this MmcM^c_mMmc​. Then, we say that M⊨∃x,F(x)M \models \exists x, F(x)M⊨∃x,F(x) iff there exists an m∈Mm \in Mm∈M such that Mmc⊨F(c)M^c_m \models F(c)Mmc​⊨F(c).
