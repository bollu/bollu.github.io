§ Crash course on DCPO: formalizing lambda calculus

In lambda calculus, we often see functions of the form λx→x(x)\lambda x \rightarrow x(x)λx→x(x). We would like a way to associate a "natural" mathematical object to such a function. The most obvious choice for lambda calculus is to try to create a set VVV of values which contains its own function space: (V→V)⊆V(V \rightarrow V) \subseteq V(V→V)⊆V. This seems to ask for a set whose cardinality is such that ∣V∣∣V∣=∣V∣|V|^|V| = |V|∣V∣∣V∣=∣V∣, which is only possible if ∣V∣=1|V| = 1∣V∣=1, ie VVV is the trivial set {∗}\{ * \}{∗}. However, we know that lambda calculus has at least two types of functions: functions that terminate and those that don't terminate. Hence the trivial set is not a valid solution. However, there is a way out. The crucial insight is one that I shall explain by analogy:
  • We can see that the cardinality of R\mathbb RR is different from the cardinality of the space of functions over it, R→R\mathbb R \rightarrow \mathbb RR→R.
  • However, "the set of all functions" isn't really something mathematicians consider. One would most likely consider "the set of all continuous functions" R→R\mathbb R \rightarrow \mathbb RR→R.
  • Now note that a function that is continuous over the reals is determined by its values at the rationals . So, rather than giving me a continus function f:R→Rf: \mathbb R \rightarrow \mathbb Rf:R→R, you can give me a continuous function f′:Q→Rf': \mathbb Q \rightarrow \mathbb Rf′:Q→R which I can Cauchy-complete, to get a function completion(f′):R→R=f\texttt{completion}(f') : \mathbb R \rightarrow \mathbb R = fcompletion(f′):R→R=f.
  • Now, cardinality considerations tell us that:
∣RQ∣=(2ℵ0)ℵ0=2ℵ0⋅ℵ0=20ℵ=∣R∣|\mathbb R^\mathbb Q| = (2^{\aleph_0})^{\aleph_0} = 2^{\aleph_0 \cdot \aleph_0} = 2^\aleph_0 = |R|∣RQ∣=(2ℵ0​)ℵ0​=2ℵ0​⋅ℵ0​=20ℵ​=∣R∣
  • We've won! We have a space R\mathbb RR whose space of continuous functions R→R\mathbb R \rightarrow \mathbb RR→R is isomorphic to R\mathbb RR.
  • We bravely posit: all functions computed by lambda-calculus are continuous! Very well. This leaves us two questions to answer to answer: (1) over what space? (2) with what topology? The answers are (1) a space of partial orders (2) with the Scott topology

§ Difference between DCPO theory and Domain theory

  • A DCPO (directed-complete partial order) is an algebraic structure that can be satisfied by some partial orders. This definition ports 'continuity' to partial orders.
  • A domain is an algebraic structure of even greater generality than a DCPO. This attempts to capture the fundamental notion of 'finitely approximable'.
  • The presentation of a domain is quite messy. The nicest axiomatization of domains that I know of is in terms of information systems . One can find an introduction to these in the excellent book 'Introduction to Order Theory' by Davey and Priestly

§ Computation as fixpoints of continuous functions

§ Posets, (least) upper bounds

  • A partial order (P,≤)(P, \leq)(P,≤) is a set equipped with a reflexive, transitive, relation ≤\leq≤ such that x≤yx \leq yx≤y and y≤xy \leq xy≤x implies x=yx = yx=y.
  • A subset D⊆PD \subseteq PD⊆P is said to have an upper bound uD∈Pu_D \in PuD​∈P iff for all d∈Dd \in Dd∈D, it is true that d≤uDd \leq u_Dd≤uD​.
  • An upper bound is essentially a cone over the subset DDD in the category PPP.
  • A subset D⊆PD \subseteq PD⊆P has a least upper bound lDl_DlD​ iff (1) lDl_DlD​ is an upper bound of DDD, and (2) for every upper bound uDu_DuD​, it is true that lD≤uDl_D \leq u_DlD​≤uD​
  • Least upper bounds are unique, since they are essentially limits of the set DDD when DDD is taken as a thin category

§ Directed Sets

  • A subset D⊆PD \subseteq PD⊆P is said to be directed iff for every finite subset S⊆DS \subseteq DS⊆D, SSS has a upper bound dSd_S dS​ in DDD. That is, the set DDD is closed under the upper bound of all of its subsets.
  • Topologically, we can think of it as being closure , since the upper bound is sort of a "limit point" of the subset, and we are saying that DDD has all of its limit points.
  • Categorically, this means that D⊆PD \subseteq PD⊆P taken as a subcategory of PPP has cones over all finite subsets. (See that we do not ask for limits/least upper bounds over all finite subsets, only cones/upper bounds).
  • We can think of the condition as saying that the information in DDD is internally consistent: for any two facts, there is a third fact that "covers" them both.
  • slogan: internal consistency begets an external infinite approximation.

  • QUESTION: why upper bounds in DDD? why not external upper bounds in PPP?
  • QUESTION: why only upper bounds in DDD? why not least upper bounds in DDD?
  • ANSWER: I think the point is that as long as we ask for upper bounds, we can pushforward via a function fff, since the image of a directed set will be directed.

§ Directed Complete Partial Order ( DCPO)

  • A poset is said to be directed complete iff every directed set D⊆PD \subseteq PD⊆P has a least upper bound in PPP.
  • Compare to chain complete, CCPO, where every chain has a LUB.
  • QUESTION: why not postulate that the least upper bound must be in DDD?
  • In a DCPO, for a directed set DDD, denote the upper bound by ∪D\cup D∪D.

§ Monotonicity and Continuity

  • A function f:P→Qf: P \to Qf:P→Q between posets is said to be monotone iff p≤p′p \leq p'p≤p′ implies that f(p)≤f(p′)f(p) \leq f(p')f(p)≤f(p′).
  • A function f:P→Qf: P \to Qf:P→Q is continuous, iff for every directed set D⊆PD \subseteq PD⊆P, it is true that f(∪D)=∪f(D)f(\cup D) = \cup f(D)f(∪D)=∪f(D).
  • This has the subtle claim that the image of a directed set is directed.

§ Continuous function

  • A function is continous if it is monotone and preserves all LUBs. This is only sensible as a definition on ccpos, because the equation defining it is: lub . f = f . lub, where lub: chain(P) \rightarrow P. However, for lubto always exist, we need P to be a CCPO. So, the definition of continuous only works for CCPOs.
  • The composition of continuous functions of chain-complete partially ordered sets is continuous.

§ ≤\leq≤ as implication

We can think of b≤ab \leq ab≤a as b  ⟹  ab \implies ab⟹a. That is, bbb has more information than aaa, and hence implies aaa.
